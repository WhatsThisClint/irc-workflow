# Scientific Tenets — Diagnostic Questions

Apply all five to every inception report. For each, reach a verdict (strong / adequate / weak / absent) backed by specific textual evidence, not a generic impression.

## 1. Objectivity
- Are claims backed by evidence/citations, or by assertion and personal conviction ("we believe", "it is clear that")?
- Is there a stated positionality or reflexivity note where the researcher's own stake in the outcome could bias data collection or interpretation (common in program evaluation of one's own org's intervention)?
- Are competing explanations for the expected outcome considered, or does the report only marshal evidence for the preferred hypothesis?
- Is the research question framed neutrally, or does its phrasing presuppose the answer (e.g. "How does X improve Y" instead of "Does X affect Y, and how")?

## 2. Validity
- **Construct validity**: do the chosen indicators/measures actually capture the theoretical construct they claim to (e.g. does "number of meetings attended" really measure "civic participation")?
- **Internal validity**: if causal claims are made, are alternative explanations (confounders, selection effects, reverse causality) addressed?
- **External validity**: is the claimed generalizability of findings proportionate to the sample/site (e.g. one district's FGDs generalized to "women's empowerment" broadly)?
- Are operational definitions given for every key term/variable, or are they left implicit?

## 3. Reliability
- Are data collection instruments (survey, FGD guide, coding scheme) described in enough detail that a second researcher could apply them the same way?
- If multiple researchers/enumerators are involved, is there any inter-rater reliability plan (double coding, calibration)?
- Are measurement tools piloted, validated, or borrowed from an established, tested source — or newly invented without testing?

## 4. Transparency
- Are data sources, sample size, and sampling method explicitly stated (not just "farmers were interviewed")?
- Are analytical choices justified (why this coding scheme, why this statistical test, why this cutoff)?
- Is it clear what data will be shared/archived and in what form?
- Are limitations and scope conditions stated openly, or only implied?

## 5. Replicability
- Could an independent researcher, given the same methods section, run the same study elsewhere/at another time and know exactly what to do?
- Is the sampling frame and recruitment process specific enough to replicate (vs. "convenience sample of available households")?
- For quantitative work: is the analysis code/plan specified enough to be re-run? For qualitative work: is the coding/analysis process specified enough to be re-applied by someone else?

## Common failure pattern to flag
A report can look rigorous while conflating these — e.g. a highly transparent methods section that is nonetheless invalid (measuring the wrong thing very precisely). Always assess the five independently; don't let strength in one imply strength in another.
