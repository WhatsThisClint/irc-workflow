# Literature Review as Argument — Critique Guide

## Summary-mode vs. argument-mode
**Summary-mode** (weak): "Smith (2019) found X. Jones (2020) found Y. Patel (2021) studied Z." — a list of findings with no throughline. Common failure of first-time researchers.

**Argument-mode** (target): the literature is marshaled to make a specific claim — "the dominant framework (A) explains outcome patterns in context type 1, but doesn't account for [specific feature of this study's context]; framework B, drawn from [cite], better predicts the mechanism at play here, which is why this study adopts B's variables and design."

When reviewing, explicitly state which mode the lit review is in, with 1-2 quoted/paraphrased examples as evidence (per copyright rules: paraphrase, don't reproduce verbatim text).

## What argument-mode requires, checked one by one
1. **A clear position**: does the lit review say what the researcher believes and why, or just what others found?
2. **Engagement with competing/opposing frameworks**: is there a plausible alternative theoretical lens that would predict a different outcome or prescribe a different method — and does the report engage it, or ignore it? (If the report only cites supportive literature, that's a red flag for confirmation bias / weak objectivity.)
3. **Explicit gap statement**: does the lit review identify a specific, real gap this study fills — not a generic "more research is needed" but something like "existing studies measure X only via proxy Y; this study uses direct measure Z" or "prior work is cross-sectional; this uses panel data to address the mediation question"?
4. **Justification for methodological choice**: does the report say *why* its method follows from the literature/theory (a design implied by the theoretical framework), separately from *feasibility* reasons (time, budget, access)? Both are legitimate, but they should be labeled as different kinds of justification — a report that only gives feasibility reasons for a major design choice has a weaker theoretical argument.
5. **Citation quality**: are citations specific (named studies, not "literature suggests")? Do they support the specific claim attached to them, or are they used as decorative authority? Flag any citation that seems vague, unverifiable, or mismatched to the claim it's attached to — but don't assume fabrication; just flag for the PM to verify.

## Anticipated critique — what to generate
For the report's chosen conceptual framework AND chosen methodology, generate the most likely critiques a reviewer in that discipline would raise:
- Theoretical: "why this framework and not [named alternative]?"
- Methodological: sampling, measurement, causal identification concerns
- Feasibility/scope: is the claimed scope of conclusions larger than the design can support?
Then check: does the report already anticipate and address any of these? If yes, credit it. If a major likely critique is entirely unaddressed, name it explicitly as a gap — this is often the single most useful thing a PM can take back to the researcher.
