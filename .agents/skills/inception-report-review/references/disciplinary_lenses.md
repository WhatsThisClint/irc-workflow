# Disciplinary Lens — Identification Method

Do not treat this file as a fixed menu of lenses to choose between. Every report belongs to a specific discipline or combination of sub-fields, and the lens must be derived from the report itself, not assigned from a preset pair. The worked examples at the bottom exist to show *how* to construct a lens, not to define the only two that exist.

## Step 1: Identify the discipline(s) from the report's own content
Look at:
- **The phenomenon studied** — is it behavioral/institutional, biophysical, economic, epidemiological, technological, etc.?
- **The constructs and variables used** — do they belong to a recognizable theoretical tradition (e.g. "empowerment" → gender/development studies; "backscatter coefficient" → remote sensing/hydrology; "willingness to pay" → economics)?
- **The methods deployed** — surveys/FGDs point one way, spectral index modeling another, econometric regression another, lab assays another.
- **What it cites** — the journals, frameworks, and named methodologies cited are usually the clearest signal of which field's standards the work is implicitly claiming to meet.

State the identified discipline(s) explicitly by name — be as specific as the evidence supports (e.g. "agricultural remote sensing / hydrological engineering" rather than just "science", "institutional economics of collective action" rather than just "economics").

## Step 2: Construct the lens for that discipline
For the identified discipline, work out (using general knowledge of how that field's practitioners review work, plus a search if the field's standards are unfamiliar or highly specialized):
- **What that field treats as its core rigor standards** — every field has its own version of validity/reliability concerns, but the specifics differ (e.g. construct validity and positionality in social science; measurement uncertainty and model calibration in environmental engineering; internal validity and pre-registration norms in experimental psychology; identification strategy and confounding in applied economics).
- **Common failure modes reviewers in that field watch for** — the mistakes that field's own peer reviewers most often flag.
- **Active theoretical or methodological debates** — competing frameworks or schools of thought within the discipline that a rigorous report should show awareness of.
- **Adopt the persona**: write the critique as that kind of practitioner would actually write it — using their characteristic concerns and vocabulary, not a generic checklist applied uniformly across disciplines.

## Step 3: Handle interdisciplinary or unfamiliar work
- If the report spans multiple disciplines (common in applied/program research — e.g. combining social equity measures with biophysical/hydrological indices), identify each discipline separately, apply Step 2 to each, and then treat the **integration point between them as its own risk area** — this is very often the weakest-justified part of interdisciplinary work (e.g. how a social variable is meant to interact with a biophysical one in a combined model or index).
- If the discipline is unfamiliar or highly specialized, don't fall back on a generic lens — use web search to establish what that field's actual rigor norms and common critique patterns are before writing the section, so the critique reflects real practice in that field rather than a guess.

## Worked examples (illustrative, not exhaustive)

**Social science / behavioral-institutional work**: concerns typically include construct validity of social measures (e.g. "empowerment", "participation" needing defensible, ideally validated indicators), positionality (especially when an org evaluates its own program), sampling and generalizability, qualitative rigor markers (triangulation, saturation, reflexivity, coding transparency), and engagement with competing theoretical frameworks (e.g. resource-agency-achievement models, capability approaches, intersectionality, depending on the specific topic).

**Natural science / environmental engineering / hydrology**: concerns typically include measurement uncertainty and error propagation, spatial/temporal scale mismatch between data sources and claims, model validation against ground-truth/observed data, data provenance and known limitations of remote sensing or sensor products, and engagement with established sector frameworks (e.g. IWRM principles, water security frameworks) rather than treating variable/index choices as self-justifying.

These two are common in WELL Labs' portfolio (IRC, WISER) but are examples of the method applied, not the boundaries of it — apply the same three-step process to any other discipline a report turns out to belong to (economics, public health, ecology, engineering, etc.).
